We all have performed Physics experiments in schools or universities. Among these experiments one of the experiments would be just to confirm an established physical law. For performing this, we would record the observations and substitute these values into a mathematical equation. And I am pretty sure that none of us have ever recorded the data that verfies the law “exactly”. There is always some difference between the LHS and RHS, i.e., $|LHS-RHS|=\epsilon$, unless we change the data and somehow verify the results to exactly verify the law. This will always be caught by an experienced faculty, and now you have to explain how are you able to exactly confirm the law.)

So, to show how to attempt to verify an established physical law, we take the help of the principles of uncertainty quantification and propagation to estimate the uncertainty in our result. Finally, we check whether the values we are getting are within the bounds we have calculated.

Equipments Used#

  • 5 Resistive Temperature Detectors(RTDs), with their positions as specified below:
    • Channel 1: - Inlet water temperature
    • Channel 2: - Outlet water temperature
    • Channel 3: - Inlet air temperature
    • Channel 4: - Outlet water temperature
    • Channel 5: - Temperature of water bath
  • Pump to feed hot water: -
    • Power rating - $0.25$ $\text{hp}$
    • max. flow rate - $1.2 \text{ m}^3/\text{hr}$
  • Variable frequency drive (a.k.a variate) to control the draft induced: - Rating - $220V$, $10A$
  • Dimmer Stat to control power supply to water bath heaters.
  • Water bath for heating water with a capacity of $20L$ -Induction heating rods of $2\text{kW}$ each, with their operation as follows:
    • First all 4 heaters are supplied power to bring the temperature to the desired level
    • After desired temperature has been reached, only 1 of the 4 heaters is left energised to maintain constant temperature.
  • A 3-phase induction motor to control the flow rate of water from water bath to the plate type heat exchangers.
  • An air flow channel for regulating air flow with dimensions $0.3\text{m} \times 0.3\text{m}$. The air flow that comes out of that baffle plate is turbulent.
  • Pitot tube to measure the velocity across the height of the channel.
  • Electromagnetic flow meter to measure water flow meter
  • Plate type heat exchanger for heat transfer between water and air.

Experimental Setup#

The experimental setup has fluid flow channel with a induced fan run by a motor at the end of the channel. This fan pulls the air from the other end hence establishing a induced flow in the channel. There is a water bath of a capacity of $20 L$ to which a pump is connected. The water bath has 4 water heaters of $2\text{kW}$ each. First all the 4 water heaters are switched on to heat the water to the desired temperature. To maintain the desired temperature, only 1 of the 4 water heaters remains switched on to compensate for the heat lost to the surroundings. There is an HMI which displays the various temperature readings from the RTDs mentioned in the above section. The water pump is also controlled by variate for controlling the flow rate of water to the heat exchanger.

Theoretical background#

First Law of Thermodynamics#

Various forms of energies interact with each other to change form to a different one. The first law provides the basis for studying the relationships among the various forms of energy and energy interations. Accoring to the first law of thermodynamics, energy can be neither created nor destroyed during a process; it can only change forms. Hence, every bit of energy should be accounted for during a process.

In this project we will only be examining the uncertainty in the verification of the first law for a control volume, hence we will discuss the first law for a control volume, and how it will be applied to the setup shown above. Now the first law for the open flow systems is as follows:

\[ \begin{equation}\dot E_{\text{in}} - \dot E_{\text{out}} = \dot E_{\text{CV}} \implies E_{\text{in}} - E_{\text{out}} = \Delta E_{\text{CV}}\end{equation}\] Here, $$ \begin{equation} \begin{aligned} E_{\text{in}} &= Q_\text{in} + W_\text{in} + m_{\text{in}}e_{\text{in}} \\ E_{\text{out}} &= Q_\text{out} + W_\text{out} + m_{\text{out}}e_{\text{out}} \end{aligned} \end{equation}$$ Substituting $(2)$ in $(1)$ and rearranging, we get the following expression for first law applied to a control volume in unsteady state.

$$ \begin{equation}Q_{\text{in,net}} - W_{\text{out,net}} + \sum_{i} (m_{\text{in},i}e_{\text{in},i}-m_{\text{out},i}e_{\text{out},i}) = m_2e_2 - m_1e_1 \end{equation}$$ where, $$\qquad\begin{aligned} Q_{in,net} \longrightarrow &\text{Net heat supplied to C.V.} = Q_{\text{in}} - Q_{\text{out}}\\ W_{out,net} \longrightarrow &\text{Net work done by C.V.} = W_{\text{in}} - W_{\text{out}}\\ m_{\text{in},i} \longrightarrow &\text{Mass flow rate in to the CV of the } i^\text{th} \text{ stream}\\ m_{\text{out},i} \longrightarrow &\text{Mass flow rate out of the CV of the } i^\text{th} \text{ stream}\\ e_{\text{in},i} \longrightarrow &\text{Energy associated with } i^\text{th} \text{ stream entering the CV} \\ = &h_{\text{in},i} + \frac{v_{\text{in},i}^2}{2} + gz_{\text{in},i}\\ e_{\text{out},i} \longrightarrow &\text{Energy associated with } i^\text{th} \text{ stream exiting the CV} \\ = &h_{\text{out},i} + \frac{v_{\text{out},i}^2}{2} + gz_{\text{out},i}\\ m_1 \longrightarrow &\text{Mass in C.V at time } t_1\\ m_2 \longrightarrow &\text{Mass in C.V at time } t_2\\ e_1 \longrightarrow &\text{Non flow energy associated with the C.V. at time } t_1 \\ = &u_1 + \frac{v_1^2}{2} + gz_1\\ e_2 \longrightarrow &\text{Non flow energy associated with the C.V. at time } t_2 \rightarrow t_1 + \Delta t\\ = &u_2 + \frac{v_2^2}{2} + gz_2 \end{aligned}$$

First Law applied to Experimental Setup#

The following assumptions have been made for analysing the control volume in the laboratory experimental setup:

  1. Steady state has been reached
  2. Velocity of control volume is negligible
  3. Height of inlet is same as the height of outlet (i.e. $z_{in}=z_{out}$)
  4. Perfect insulation of control volume
  5. No work supplied to the control volume
  6. Change in velocity of flui flowing through the control volume is negligible (i.e. $V_{\text{in,}i}\equiv V_{\text{out,}i}$)

The schematic reperesentation of the control volume to be analysed is as shown below:

The assumptions lead to the following: $$m_{\text{in},i}=m_{\text{out},i} \qquad m_2=m_1 \qquad e_2=e_1 $$ $$Q_\text{in,net} = 0 \qquad W_\text{out,net} = 0$$

So, modifying the equations in $(3)$ to get the following equation: $$\begin{aligned} &\cancel{Q_{\text{in,net}}} - \cancel{W_{\text{out,net}}} + \sum_{i} (m_{\text{in},i}e_{\text{in},i}-m_{\text{out},i}e_{\text{out},i}) = \cancel{m_2e_2 - m_1e_1}\\ \implies &\sum_{i\in\text{\{air,water\}}} (m_{\text{in},i}e_{\text{in},i}-m_{\text{out},i}e_{\text{out},i}) =0 \implies \sum_{i\in\text{\{air,water\}}} m_{i}(e_{\text{in},i}-e_{\text{out},i}) =0\\ \end{aligned}$$

From assumptions (6) and (3), then rearranging and differentiating throughout w.r.t time, we get $q_\text{air} = q_\text{water}$, which when extended comes out to be:

$$\begin{equation} \dot m_\text{water} C_\text{p,water}(T_\text{in,water}-T_\text{out,water}) = \dot m_\text{air} C_\text{p,air}(T_\text{out,air}-T_\text{in,air}) \end{equation}$$

The above expression translates to magnitude of heat supplied to the system by the hot fluid $(q_h)$ is the same as the heat taken away from the system by the colder fluid $(q_c)$

Experimental Uncertainty Quantification and Propagation#

Basic Statistical Theory#

When we have sampled the whole population (population size $=N$), then we can use the following expressions: $$\begin{aligned} \text{Population Mean, }\mu &= \frac{\sum\limits_{i=1}^N{x_i}}{N}\\ \text{Population Variance, }\sigma^2 &= \frac{\sum\limits_{i=1}^N(x_i-\bar x)^2}{N}\\ \text{Population Standard Deviation, }\sigma &= \sqrt{\frac{\sum\limits_{i=1}^N(x_i-\bar x)^2}{N}} \end{aligned}$$

But, in this case, sampling the whole population is practically not possible, as we can’t keep monitoring the process parameters indefinitely. Hence, we sample only a small portion of size $n$ from the population and use this data to estimate the population parameters. The unbiased estimators, of the population using the sample data is expressed as follows: $$\begin{aligned} \text{Sample Mean, }\bar X &= \sum\limits_{i=1}^n\frac{x_i}{n}\\ \text{Sample Variance, }s^2 &= \sum\limits_{i=1}^n\frac{(x_i-\bar x)^2}{n-1}\\ \text{Sample Standard Deviation, }s &= \sqrt{\sum\limits_{i=1}^n\frac{(x_i-\bar x)^2}{n-1}} \end{aligned}$$

For understanding, why we consider $s = \sqrt{\sum\limits_{i=1}^n\frac{(x_i-\bar x)^2}{n-1}}$ as an unbiased estimator of population standard estimator, you can refer to the this blogpost for further understanding of the underlying principles.

We also know, $\text{Var}(\bar X)=\sigma^2_{\bar X} = \dfrac{\text{Var}(X)}{n} = \dfrac{\sigma_X^2}{n}\implies \sigma_{\bar X}=\dfrac{\sigma_{X}}{\sqrt n}$

Experimental uncertainty propagation#

We know, if $X_1, X_2, \ldots, X_n$ are random variables whose uncertainties $\sigma_{X_1}, \sigma_{X_2}, \ldots, \sigma_{X_n}$ are small, and if $R = f(X_1, X_2, \ldots, X_n)$, then the absolute uncertainty in $R$ is given as

$$\sigma_R = \sqrt{\underbrace{\left(\dfrac{\partial R}{\partial X_1}\right)^2 \sigma_{X_1}^2 + \left(\dfrac{\partial R}{\partial X_2}\right)^2 \sigma_{X_2}^2 + \cdots + \left(\dfrac{\partial R}{\partial X_n}\right)^2 \sigma_{X_n}^2}_\text{Square terms} + \underbrace{\sum\limits_{i,j}\dfrac{\partial R}{\partial X_i}\dfrac{\partial R}{\partial X_j}\sigma_{X_i X_j}}_\text{Covariance terms}}$$

The above relation comes from the following Taylor expansion of $R(X_1, X_2, \ldots, X_n)$, where $f$ is the calculated value and $X_1,X_2, \ldots X_n$ are the true values of the random varaiables that are being sampled. The expansion is only till the linear terms, as the distribution of data can be assumed to be approximating the calculated value well within the linear approximation range. $$R = R^0 + \sum\limits_{i=1}^n{\dfrac{\partial R}{\partial X_i}(X_i-\mu_i)}$$ $\begin{aligned} \text{where, }\quad \mu_i \longrightarrow &\text{population mean of } X_i\\ R^0 \longrightarrow &\text{true value of R calculated at } X_1, X_2, \ldots X_n \end{aligned}$

Then, the variance in $R$ can be calculated as follows: $$\text{Var}(R) = \text{Var}(R^0) + \text{Var}\left(\sum\limits_{i=1}^{n}\left[\left(\dfrac{\partial R}{\partial X_i}\right)(X_i-\mu_i)\right]\right)$$

Since, $\text{Var}(R^0)$, $\text{Var}(\mu_i) = 0$ as these are true values, hence, we get the following expression for uncertainty propagation in a calculated variable.

$$\text{Var}(R) = \sum\limits_{i=1}^{n}\left(\dfrac{\partial R}{\partial X_i}\right)^2\text{Var}(X_i) + \sum\limits_{i=1}^{n-1}\sum\limits_{j\ge i}^{n}\dfrac{\partial R}{\partial X_i}\dfrac{\partial R}{\partial X_j}\text{Var}(X_i)\text{Var}(X_j)\\ + \sum\limits_{i=1}^{n-2}\sum\limits_{j\ge i}^{n-1}\sum\limits_{k\ge j}^{n}\dfrac{\partial R}{\partial X_i}\dfrac{\partial R}{\partial X_j}\dfrac{\partial R}{\partial X_k}\text{Var}(X_i)\text{Var}(X_j)\text{Var}(X_k) + \ldots$$

But, here we will assume that there’s no correlation between the variables $X_i$. Whether this assumption will make the result incorrect needs to be examined. This can be done in a future blogpost. Hence, we will have the uncertainty propagation relation as follows: $$\begin{equation} \sigma_R = \sqrt{\left(\dfrac{\partial R}{\partial X_1}\right)^2 \sigma_{X_1}^2 + \left(\dfrac{\partial R}{\partial X_2}\right)^2 \sigma_{X_2}^2 + \cdots + \left(\dfrac{\partial R}{\partial X_n}\right)^2 \sigma_{X_n}^2} \end{equation}$$

To account for the error in predicting the uncertainty when removing the covariance terms in the above equation, one can go through this highly cited paper “On the Exact Variance of Products” by Leo A. Goodman.

Consider the data reduction equation (DRE), as shown below $$R = X_1^a X_2^b X_3^c$$ According to equation $(5)$. The absolute uncertainty of $R$ can be written as $$\sigma_R \approx \sqrt{\left(a\dfrac{R}{X_1}\right)^2 \sigma_{X_1}^2 + \left(b\dfrac{R}{X_2}\right)^2 \sigma_{X_2}^2 + \left(c\dfrac{R}{X_3}\right)^2 \sigma_{X_3}^2}$$

The relative uncertainty is calculated as $\dfrac{\sigma_R}{R}$. Hence, the expression for the above shown DRE comes to be,

$$\dfrac{\sigma_R}{R} = \sqrt{\left(\dfrac{a}{X_1}\right)^2 \sigma_{X_1}^2 + \left(\dfrac{b}{X_2}\right)^2 \sigma_{X_2}^2 + \left(\dfrac{c}{X_3}\right)^2 \sigma_{X_3}^2}$$

Now, since we have to verify equation $(4)$, hence we need to find the uncertainties in $q_c$ and $q_h$.

We have, $$\begin{aligned} q_c &= \dot m_\text{air} C_\text{p,air}(T_\text{out,air}-T_\text{in,air})\\ q_h &= \dot m_\text{water} C_\text{p,water}(T_\text{in,water}-T_\text{out,water}) \end{aligned}$$

So, we find the uncertainty for the general case $q = \dot m C_p (T_\text{in}-T_\text{out}) = \dot m C_p\Delta T$. The absolute uncertainty, according to equation $(7)$ comes out to be: $$\begin{equation} \sigma_q = q\times\sqrt{\left(\frac{1}{\dot m}\right)^2 \sigma_{\dot m}^2+ \left(\frac{1}{C_p}\right)^2 \sigma_{C_p}^2 + \left(\frac{1}{\Delta T}\right)^2 \sigma_{\Delta T}^2} \end{equation}$$ From first principles, we have $$\sigma_{\Delta T}^2 = \left(\dfrac{\partial \Delta T}{\partial T_\text{in}}\sigma_{T_\text{in}}\right)^2 + \left(\dfrac{\partial \Delta T}{\partial T_\text{out}}\sigma_{T_\text{out}}\right)^2$$

Since, $\Delta T = (T_\text{in} - T_\text{out})$, the above equation becomes, $$\begin{equation} \sigma_{\Delta T}^2 = \sigma_{T_\text{in}}^2 + \sigma_{T_\text{out}}^2 \end{equation}$$ Substituting expression $(9)$ in $(7)$, we get $$\sigma_q = q\times\sqrt{\left(\frac{1}{\dot m}\right)^2 \sigma_{\dot m}^2+ \left(\frac{1}{C_p}\right)^2 \sigma_{C_p}^2 + \left(\frac{1}{\Delta T}\right)^2 \sigma_{T_\text{in}}^2 + + \left(\frac{1}{\Delta T}\right)^2 \sigma_{T_\text{out}}^2}$$

We can consider the unceratinty in determination of $C_p$ to be negligible. This can be attributed to studying the variation of $C_p$ over huge number of experiments, hence we know which value of $C_p$ to use under each temperature range. Hence, $\sigma_{C_p}\approx 0$. Hence, the uncertainty in heat transfer can be written as $$ \sigma_q = q\times\sqrt{\left(\frac{1}{\dot m}\right)^2 \sigma_{\dot m}^2+ \left(\frac{1}{\Delta T}\right)^2 \sigma_{T_\text{in}}^2 + \left(\frac{1}{\Delta T}\right)^2 \sigma_{T_\text{out}}^2} $$

The relative uncertainty can be written as $$\begin{equation} \dfrac{\sigma_q}{q} = \sqrt{\left(\frac{1}{\dot m}\right)^2 \sigma_{\dot m}^2+ \left(\frac{1}{\Delta T}\right)^2 \sigma_{T_\text{in}}^2 + \left(\frac{1}{\Delta T}\right)^2 \sigma_{T_\text{out}}^2} \end{equation}$$

Hence, according to equation $(8)$ we can get the unceratinty in determination of $q_\text{air}$ and $q_\text{water}$ as follows $$\begin{equation} \begin{aligned} \dfrac{\sigma_{q_\text{c}}}{q_{c}} &= \sqrt{\left(\frac{1}{\dot m_\text{air}}\right)^2 \sigma_{\dot m_\text{air}}^2+ \left(\frac{1}{(T_\text{out,air}-T_\text{in,air})}\right)^2 \cdot\left(\sigma_{T_\text{in,air}}^2 + \sigma_{T_\text{out,air}}^2\right)}\newline \dfrac{\sigma_{q_\text{h}}}{q_{h}} &= \sqrt{\left(\frac{1}{\dot m_\text{water}}\right)^2 \sigma_{\dot m_\text{water}}^2+ \left(\frac{1}{(T_\text{in,water}-T_\text{out,water})}\right)^2 \cdot \left(\sigma_{T_\text{in,water}}^2 + \sigma_{T_\text{out,water}}^2\right)} \end{aligned} \end{equation}$$

Now, since $\dot m_\text{air} = \rho_\text{air}u_\text{air}A_\text{channel}$ and $\dot m_\text{water} = \rho_\text{water}\dot Q_\text{water}$, hence, we get the following relation:

$$\begin{aligned} \frac{\sigma_{\dot m_\text{air}}}{\dot m_\text{air}} &= \sqrt{\left(\frac{\sigma_{\rho_\text{air}}}{\rho_\text{air}}\right)^2+\left(\frac{\sigma_{u_\text{air}}}{u_\text{air}}\right)^2+\left(\frac{\sigma_{A_\text{channel}}}{A_\text{channel}}\right)^2}\\ \frac{\sigma_{\dot m_\text{water}}}{\dot m_\text{water}} &= \sqrt{\left(\frac{\sigma_{\rho_\text{water}}}{\rho_\text{water}}\right)^2+\left(\frac{\sigma_{\dot Q_\text{water}}}{\dot Q_\text{water}}\right)^2} \end{aligned}$$

Since, we have assumed $\rho_\text{water}$, $\rho_\text{air}$, $A_\text{channel}$ are constants, hence we get the following

$$\begin{aligned} \frac{\sigma_{\dot m_\text{air}}}{\dot m_\text{air}} &= \frac{\sigma_{u_\text{air}}}{u_\text{air}}\\ \frac{\sigma_{\dot m_\text{water}}}{\dot m_\text{water}} &= \frac{\sigma_{\dot Q_\text{water}}}{\dot Q_\text{water}} \end{aligned}$$

Hence, equation $(9)$ becomes, $$\begin{equation} \begin{aligned} \dfrac{\sigma_{q_\text{c}}}{q_{c}} &= \sqrt{\left(\frac{1}{\dot u_\text{air}}\right)^2 \sigma_{\dot u_\text{air}}^2+ \left(\frac{1}{(T_\text{out,air}-T_\text{in,air})}\right)^2 \cdot\left(\sigma_{T_\text{in,air}}^2 + \sigma_{T_\text{out,air}}^2\right)}\newline \dfrac{\sigma_{q_\text{h}}}{q_{h}} &= \sqrt{\left(\frac{1}{\dot Q_\text{water}}\right)^2 \sigma_{\dot Q_\text{water}}^2+ \left(\frac{1}{(T_\text{in,water}-T_\text{out,water})}\right)^2 \cdot \left(\sigma_{T_\text{in,water}}^2 + \sigma_{T_\text{out,water}}^2\right)} \end{aligned} \end{equation}$$

Accounting Heat Balance Error#

As mentioned earlier, we know that first law of thermodynamics is pretty difficult to confirm, hence we confirm it within some level of condfidence. So to account for this we define Heat Balance Error (HBE), which can eb defined as follows: $$HBE = \dfrac{q_h-q_c}{q_c}\times 100 = \left(\dfrac{q_h}{q_c}-1\right)\times 100$$ Hence, for verifying the first law of thermodynamics, $\sigma_\text{HBE}$ needs to be within some confidence interval (usually $95\%$ confidence level). So, we quantify the uncertainty in HBE as follows: $$\sigma_\text{HBE} = \sqrt{\left(\dfrac{\partial (\text{HBE})}{\partial q_h}\right)^2\sigma_{q_h}^2 + \left(\dfrac{\partial (\text{HBE})}{\partial q_c}\right)^2\sigma_{q_c}^2} = \sqrt{\left(\frac{1}{q_c}\right)^2\sigma_{q_h}^2 + \left(\dfrac{-q_h}{q_c^2}\right)^2\sigma_{q_c}^2}$$ $$\implies \frac{\sigma_\text{HBE}}{\text{HBE}} = \sqrt{\frac{1}{(q_h-q_c)^2}\sigma_{q_h}^2 + \frac{q_h^2}{(q_h-q_c)^2}\sigma_{q_c}^2}$$
